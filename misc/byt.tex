\chapter{Beirdd y Tywysogion}
The poetry composed by the \mw{Beirdd y Tywysogion}, or Poets of the Princes, comprises the work of a great variety of poets geographically. These poets lived over a period of several hundred years from eleventh century until the fall of the principality of Gwynedd to the English in 1284.

\mwengtr[beiron]{CBT~VII:~31,~ll.~1--4}{Pei kawn o gyflwr gyfle broui --- rin,\\Kyt bei ron vyng krogi,\\Vy neges oed vynegi\\Vyn gouec, dyn tec, y ti.}{If we have in this state a chance to experience --- intimacy\\Even if I were hanged \\My message would be to proclaim\\My affection, pretty one, to you.}

Although lenition of \mw{r} is not written in the Middle Welsh period, the \textit{cynghanedd} makes a suggestion as to lenition of \mw{ron}: \mw{ron} alliterates with \mw{rin} `intimacy' here, making a \textit{cynghanedd draws}. There is no reason for \mw{rin} to be lenited here, so non-lenition of \mw{ron} would make for better alliteration. However, maybe this example must be considered a research exception, as \mw{bei ron} may be considered one word \parencite[s.v.\ \textit{perhôn, peirhôn, pei rhôn, pe rhôn, \&c.}]{bevan_geiriadur_2014}. This would make lenition or lack of lenition dependant on internal sandhi rules rather lenition following \ei.

\mwcc[oesvrenhin]{CBT~VII:~41,~l.~10}{Nyt oes, Vrenhin, Deus domin, dimynn eisseu.}{There is not, King, Lord God, any wish.}

The translation \ref{oesvrenhin} suggests that this is a research exception, since \mw{Vrenhin} `king' does not serve as a complement to \mw{oes}. Rather, it is lenited because of its use as a vocative.
\mwcc[oeswadeu]{CBT~VII:~41,~l.~30}{Geyr bronn Douyd a Ssywedyd, nyt oes wadeu;}{In presence of the Lord and teacher, there are no contradictions;}

Lenition of \mw{wadeu} following \oes\ in Example \ref{oeswadeu} is confirmed both on the basis of orthography and \textit{cynghanedd}. It is a \textit{cynghanedd sain}, where \mw{Douyd} `Lord' rhymes with \mw{Ssywedyd} `teacher', which in turn also alliterates with \mw{(s-) wadeu} `contradictions'.
